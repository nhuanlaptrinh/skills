# Viet Bai OpenClaw

## Core Workflow

1. Read `references/program.md` for the offer facts, audience, capabilities, objections, support model, and date-sensitive claims.
2. Read `references/voice.md` before writing any public-facing copy.
3. Choose one story angle before drafting:
   - Busy founder/freelancer doing content, image, data, customer care, reporting, and admin work alone.
   - Office worker or operator stuck with repetitive website, Excel, Word, PDF, form, fanpage, or reporting tasks.
   - Person who uses ChatGPT but still has to copy, paste, click, download, upload, and check everything by hand.
   - Learner who wants automation but gets stuck because tools are too generic and do not match their real workflow.
   - Older or low-tech learner who thinks AI automation is only for technical people.
   - Perfectionist who keeps tweaking prompts and tools but never gets a working assistant.
4. Draft from a lived work scene first, then introduce the method: create personal AI assistants with OpenClaw, each assistant has a job, then train and improve them by using them on real work.
5. End with one direct action: register for OpenClaw (Link: `https://oplw.anhlaptrinh.vn/`), message for advice, or send the reader's workflow so it can be mapped into assistants.

## Writing Rules

- Write in Vietnamese.
- Prioritize story-driven copy over feature listing.
- Sound like a practical older-brother guide: direct, conversational, slightly blunt, not polished-corporate.
- Use "anh em" naturally for Facebook/Zalo posts; use "bạn" when writing landing page copy, email, or more personal objection handling.
- Keep the main promise practical: create a team of AI assistants on the user's own computer to reduce repetitive digital work.
- Contrast ChatGPT and OpenClaw clearly:
  - ChatGPT mostly answers in chat.
  - OpenClaw receives commands and performs actions on the computer.
- Keep the assistant-team idea clear:
  - Many assistants can exist at once.
  - Each assistant owns one job.
  - The user tags or calls the right assistant and gives instructions.
  - Assistants can keep separate workspaces/history.
- Use concrete jobs: website login, data collection, Excel analysis, report generation, Word/PDF reading, form filling, fanpage replies, content/image creation, posting schedules, customer information lookup, simple website/domain deployment, and workflow automation.
- Frame OpenClaw like hiring a new assistant: it will not be perfect from day one; let it run first, then improve instructions, data, and workflow.
- Emphasize the learning method: "vướng đâu gỡ đó", no theory-heavy start, make it work first, optimize later.
- Mention support only as practical help: video lessons, Zalo, Zoom, UltraViewer, and direct guidance when stuck.
- Use proof/offer points only if relevant and verify date-sensitive details before publishing.
- Do not promise effortless success, guaranteed income, or a perfect assistant. Make clear that the learner still needs to think, test, and apply the tool to their own work.

## Output Shapes

For a Facebook/Zalo post:
- Hook with a real scene or blunt belief.
- Tell a short personal/work story.
- Explain why normal AI chat is not enough.
- Bridge to OpenClaw as a team of assistants that act on the computer.
- Handle one fear or objection.
- Close with a direct CTA.

For an email:
- Subject line in the same voice, practical and non-clickbait.
- Short opening scene.
- One belief shift about AI automation.
- OpenClaw bridge.
- Clear CTA.

For a landing page section:
- Use short, direct headings.
- Keep details specific and workflow-based.
- Avoid startup-style hype, vague AI buzzwords, and shiny corporate language.

For a video script:
- Open with a relatable pain.
- Show the before/after of manual work vs commanding an assistant.
- Explain one capability with an example.
- Close by inviting the viewer to register or send their workflow.

## Guardrails

- Do not copy long passages from the source website. Preserve the voice through the patterns in `voice.md`, not by pasting source paragraphs.
- Do not make OpenClaw sound like a generic chatbot course. The key is computer-use automation and personal assistant workflows.
- Avoid generic education-ad phrases: no "unlock your potential", "master the future", "world-class curriculum", or vague "AI revolution" hype.
- Keep bluntness purposeful. Do not overdo shock lines or profanity.
- If the user asks for price, guarantee, current bonus, or "today" claims, verify against the latest source before publishing.
